How to Write a YouTube Shorts Script: The Formula That Works in 2026

You have 3 seconds before the algorithm decides whether to push or bury your Short. Here's the script structure that changes everything — with 10 ready-made templates.

Ninety percent of Shorts that die under 500 views share the same problem.

It's not camera quality. It's not a lack of followers. It's not the posting time.

It's the script. Or the lack of one.

Creators who record on the fly think they're being authentic. In practice, they're being confusing. And the YouTube algorithm has zero patience for confusion — it simply stops distributing the video.

This article teaches you the script structure that makes Shorts go viral. With a formula, templates, and real examples. No fluff.

Why scripting matters more in Shorts than in long-form video

It sounds counterintuitive. Shorts are short — 30 to 60 seconds. How much scripting do you really need for 60 seconds?

More than you'd think.

In a 10-minute long-form video, you have room for error. If one section is weak, the viewer can hang on and wait for the next good moment. In a 45-second Short, every second counts. Every word needs to be in the right place. Every pause needs to be intentional.

In 2026, YouTube evaluates something called User Satisfaction Score — a metric that combines watch time, replays, shares, and comments to decide whether to push your Short to more people. A poorly structured script tanks that score within the first 7 seconds.

Seven seconds. That's all you have before the algorithm decides your video doesn't deserve distribution.

The anatomy of a Short that works

Every viral Short has four parts. Doesn't matter the niche, the creator, or the format. Those four parts are always there.

Part 1: The Hook (0 to 3 seconds)

The hook is the only part of the script that truly drives initial distribution. If you lose the viewer here, nothing else matters.

There are five types of hooks that consistently work:

  • Curiosity hook: "What happens when you do this for 30 days?"
  • Contradiction hook: "Everything you've been taught about this is wrong."
  • Result hook: Show the end result before explaining the process. The viewer stays to find out how you got there.
  • Identification hook: "If you've ever done this, this video is for you."
  • Urgency hook: "Before you do anything tomorrow morning, watch this."

The most common mistake: a generic hook. "Hey guys, today I'm going to talk about..." That's not a hook. It's an invitation to scroll to the next video.

Part 2: The Context (3 to 10 seconds)

After the hook, the viewer stuck around. Now they need a reason to keep watching.

Context answers: why does this matter to me?

It's not about you. It's about the viewer. "I did this for 30 days" isn't context. "I did this for 30 days and lost 18 lbs without a gym" is context — because the benefit is spelled out.

Context should be one sentence, two at most. If you're over-explaining at this point, you don't have a clear enough idea of what your video is actually delivering.

Part 3: The Content (10 to 50 seconds)

This is where the real value lives. Delivering on the promise you made in the hook.

Rules for this section:

  • One idea per Short. If you're trying to cover three things, you have three Shorts, not one.
  • Cuts every 3–5 seconds. A static shot for more than 5 seconds in a Short is a dead shot. Visual rhythm needs to be constant.
  • On-screen text complements, it doesn't repeat. If you're saying "the first step is…" and the text on screen says "step one:", you're using two channels to say the same thing. Use text to add information, not to be redundant.
  • Specific beats generic every time. "Drink more water" is generic. "Drink 16 oz before breakfast" is specific and actionable.

Part 4: The Ending (last 5–10 seconds)

Most creators end their Shorts with "so that's it, hope this helped." That ending kills engagement.

The ending should do one thing: trigger an action or a strong emotion.

Endings that work:

  • A reveal that reframes everything that came before
  • A question that invites the viewer to comment
  • A cliffhanger that teases the next video
  • A closing line that wraps up the key takeaway in one memorable sentence

What doesn't work: robotic subscribe-begging. "If you liked this, subscribe and drop a like." That's become pure noise — nobody even registers it anymore.

The complete 4-part formula

Putting it all together, the structure looks like this:

[HOOK] — grabs attention in under 3 seconds
[CONTEXT] — explains why it matters in under 7 seconds
[CONTENT] — delivers the value you promised
[ENDING] — closes with impact or a call to action

It looks simple because it is. The difficulty isn't in understanding the formula — it's in executing each part with precision.

10 ready-made script templates for any niche

Each template below works regardless of your niche. Replace the brackets with your specific content.

Template 1: The Common Mistake

Hook: "[X%] of people are doing this wrong."
Context: "And it's costing them [concrete consequence]."
Content: Show the mistake. Explain why it's wrong. Show the correct version.
Ending: "Now you have no excuse."

Template 2: The Revealed Result

Hook: Show the end result without context (impactful visual)
Context: "This took [time] and [specific effort]."
Content: The process in quick steps
Ending: "Want to learn how? Part 2 tomorrow."

Template 3: The Comparison

Hook: "A vs. B. Most people pick wrong."
Context: "I tested both for [period]. The result surprised me."
Content: Present A, present B, compare using a specific criterion
Ending: A direct verdict with no hedging

Template 4: The Insider Secret

Hook: "What [specific group] knows that you don't."
Context: "[Profession/group] uses this every day. Most people have no idea."
Content: The specific technique, tool, or approach
Ending: "Now you know too. Use it."

Template 5: The Before and After

Hook: Show the "after" in the first 2 seconds
Context: "[Time] ago, this was the before."
Content: Show the before. Explain what changed and why.
Ending: "The difference was [specific element]."

Template 6: The Quick List

Hook: "[Number] things that [consequence]. Number [X] nobody talks about."
Context: Not needed — go straight into the list
Content: List of 3–5 items, each in 5–8 seconds
Ending: "Save this video. You'll need it."

Template 7: The Unpopular Opinion

Hook: "I'm about to say something half of you will disagree with."
Context: "But the data shows the opposite of what most people believe."
Content: The opinion + a solid argument + evidence
Ending: "Disagree? Comment below. Let's talk."

Template 8: The Lightning Tutorial

Hook: "How to [result] in [surprisingly short time]."
Context: "Without [obstacle most people think is necessary]."
Content: Step 1, Step 2, Step 3 — fast and visual
Ending: "Try it and let me know how it goes."

Template 9: The Science Fact

Hook: "Your brain does this and you don't even notice."
Context: "[Study/concrete data] proves it."
Content: Explain the phenomenon simply and visually
Ending: "Now that you know, you'll spot this everywhere."

Template 10: The 60-Second Story

Hook: "A year ago I was [difficult situation]."
Context: "Today it's different. Here's what changed."
Content: The turning point — the specific moment that changed everything
Ending: "The lesson nobody told me: [unique insight]."

The mistake that kills even the best-structured script

You can have the best script in the world and still watch the Short die. Know why?

Because a written script and a spoken script are two different things.

When you read your script out loud for the first time and it sounds too formal, too stiff, or too long — don't adjust your voice to fit the script. Adjust the script to fit your voice.

Practical test: record yourself reading the script and listen back without watching the video. If at any point you thought "nobody actually talks like that," rewrite that part.

Shorts that go viral sound like a conversation, not a presentation.

How long should your script be on paper?

A practical reference:

  • 30-second Short: 60 to 80 words in the script
  • 45-second Short: 90 to 120 words
  • 60-second Short: 120 to 150 words

If your script has more words than that, you're trying to say more than the time allows — and you'll end up rushing, rambling, or cutting important content in the edit.

Write less. Edit more. Speak slowly enough to be understood.

How to adapt a script for different niches

Template 8 (The Lightning Tutorial) applied across 4 different niches:

Fitness:
"How to gain 4 lbs of muscle in 4 weeks. No expensive supplements, no fancy gym. Just 3 exercises. Here's the exact protocol."

Cooking:
"How to make homemade bread in 40 minutes. No yeast, no kneading. 4 ingredients you already have. Let's go."

Personal Finance:
"How to save an extra $200 this month. Without cutting the things you love. One 10-minute adjustment most people overlook. Here's how it works."

Business:
"How to close your first sale in 24 hours. No product, no website, no upfront investment. The straight-to-the-point method."

Same structure. Completely different niches. Each one resonates with its specific audience.

The perfect script nobody ever uses

The most common trap for creators who study scripting: they keep rewriting until it's perfect and never actually post.

Let me be straight with you: there's no such thing as a perfect script before you publish. Scripts get good after 20, 30, 50 Shorts — when you understand which phrases land with your specific audience, which rhythm works for your speaking style, which endings generate the most comments in your niche.

Scripting is a skill. Skills are built through repetition, not perfectionism.

Grab a template from this article. Adapt it to your niche. Record it. Post it. Analyze what worked. Come back next week with one variation.

In 10 weeks you'll look back at your first Short and clearly see what was off. That clarity doesn't come from reading about scripting. It comes from doing it.
