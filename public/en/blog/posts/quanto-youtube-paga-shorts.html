The number that will change your perspective forever

I'll get straight to it: 1 million views on a Short earns somewhere between $20 and $100 for most creators.

Read that again: $20 to $100. For 1 million views.

Not $1,000. Not $5,000. Twenty to a hundred dollars.

For comparison: that same 1 million views on a long-form finance video can bring in between $5,000 and $15,000.

Yes. The gap can be up to 150 times larger.

That doesn't mean Shorts are useless. It means you need to understand what they're actually for before you build your entire strategy around them. And that's exactly what we're going to dig into here.

How Shorts payments actually work (what YouTube doesn't explain clearly)

YouTube doesn't pay directly "per view" on Shorts the way many people assume. The system involves a shared Creator Pool.

Here's how it works: YouTube runs ads between Shorts in the feed. The revenue those ads generate forms a global pool. That pool is then distributed among all monetized creators based on each creator's share of engaged views out of the global total.

In other words: you're not competing against advertisers. You're competing against every other creator in the world.

What are engaged views?

In March 2025, YouTube made an important change: it started counting any playback start as a "view" on Shorts, even without a minimum watch time. But for monetization purposes, what matters are engaged views — moments when a viewer genuinely watched and showed real interest in the video.

This distinction matters because your view counts may look higher today than before March 2025. But what actually earns money is genuine engagement.

Why is Shorts RPM so low?

1. Shorts don't have ads inside the video

Long-form videos run ads before, during, and after. Shorts only show ads between videos in the feed. The frequency is much lower.

2. The revenue pool is shared globally

With a long-form video, advertisers pay directly for placement in your content. With Shorts, you're taking a slice of a pie split among all monetized creators worldwide.

3. Licensed music cuts your revenue in half

If you use a licensed track in your Short, half your revenue goes to music licensing. Use two tracks, and you're left with only a third. Many creators use these songs without realizing the financial hit.

4. Non-original content can be fully demonetized

Since August 2025, YouTube has tightened the rules on repetitive or AI-generated content that lacks original transformation. If your Short reuses someone else's content without significant creative changes, it won't be monetized.

Real-world creator cases

Result: a global audience. Her RPM is substantially higher than average because a significant portion of her views come from high-purchasing-power countries.

Takeaway: Shorts with no spoken language have serious international RPM potential.

Jooj Natu — Shorts as a launchpad

But what did Jooj do differently? He didn't rely on Shorts for direct monetization. He used the reach to funnel subscribers into long-form videos, where RPM is in a completely different league.

The conclusion is unavoidable: Shorts are not a monetization strategy. They're a growth strategy.

What's changing in 2026: updates that affect your earnings

Restrictions on AI-generated content

Shorts created with AI that lack original transformation are being demonetized at scale. The answer isn't to stop using AI — it's to use it to speed up your original production, not replace it.

Engaged views as the central metric

For monetization, only playbacks with genuine interest count. Hook and retention have become even more critical: it's not enough for the video to be seen — it needs to be watched.

Seasonality matters more than you'd think

November and December pay significantly more. Black Friday and the holiday season drive up ad volume and pool value. Creators who post consistently throughout the year harvest the most during this window.

Tier 1 audiences can double or triple your RPM

A view from a US-based user is worth more than one from most other markets. Shorts that go viral internationally can completely flip the financial math in your favor.

How to maximize what you earn from Shorts

  • Choose the highest-RPM niche you can. The gap between comedy and finance can be 4 to 10 times.
  • Avoid licensed music. Use YouTube's free Audio Library — it's free and has no impact on monetization.
  • Ramp up in November and December. Pool value rises with Black Friday and the holiday season.
  • Focus on full watch-through. A Short with 100K views and 80% retention is worth more than one with 1M views and 10%.
  • Attract an international audience. Visual content with no language dependency has far greater RPM potential.
  • Track engaged views in Analytics. If they're well below total views, your retention needs work.

The honest conclusion

Shorts are not a primary income source for most creators in 2026. Full stop.

But they're one of the best channel-growth tools that has ever existed. Massive organic reach, low production cost, high virality potential.

The mistake creators make is measuring Shorts by what they pay directly. The right metric is: how many subscribers did this Short bring in? And how valuable are those subscribers?

The biggest Shorts creators — Zach King, Yasmin Art Drawing, Jooj Natu — didn't build their empires by relying on Shorts RPM. They built audiences. And an audience, when all is said and done, is the most valuable asset you can have on YouTube.
